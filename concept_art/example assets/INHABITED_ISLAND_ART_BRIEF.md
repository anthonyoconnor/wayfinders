# Inhabited island art brief

Long ago, the world fractured and separated its people across remote islands. Over generations, isolated communities became distinct tribes with their own architecture, materials, settlement patterns, ornament, and relationship to the sea. Exploration can reveal these lost tribes, and returning safely allows connections between them to be restored.

The candidate set uses four visual cultures:

- **High-island terrace builders:** stone stairs, rain terraces, cliff-supported timber halls, dark masonry, and warm rust-red roofing.
- **Low-cay lagoon weavers:** lightweight stilt homes, woven pale roofs, raised boardwalks, fish-drying frames, and turquoise-and-ochre textile motifs.
- **Atoll shell masons:** settlements spread across motu, coral-lime walls, open circular meeting courts, shell inlay, and white-and-indigo ornament.
- **Rocky-skerry cliff keepers:** basalt windbreaks, cliff-cut store rooms, compact timber platforms, rope access, cisterns, and muted copper-orange markings.

Settlements must remain plausible for the land available. Architecture should respond to terrain rather than covering the entire island. The islands should still retain clear beaches, vegetation, working space, and readable natural silhouettes.
